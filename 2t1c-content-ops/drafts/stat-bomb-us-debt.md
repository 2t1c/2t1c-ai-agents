The US paid $970 billion in interest on its debt last year.

Not principal. Not programs. Just interest.

That's $7,300 per household — every household in America.

For context: it's more than the average American spends on healthcare, home furnishings, gasoline, clothing, and education combined.

For every dollar the government collected in taxes, 19 cents went straight to debt service.

Interest payments consumed:
— All corporate income tax revenue
— 56% of all payroll tax revenue
— 37% of all individual income tax revenue

Interest is now the third-largest line item in the federal budget.

Behind only Social Security and Medicare.

Bigger than national defense.

In 2015, the US paid $223 billion in interest.

In 2020, $345 billion.

In 2025, $970 billion.

That's a 4x increase in 10 years.

The national debt just hit $38.4 trillion.

It's growing at $6.12 billion per day.

$255 million per hour.

$70,843 per second.

The Congressional Budget Office projects interest payments will hit $1 trillion in 2026.

Then $1.8 trillion by 2035.

Over the next decade: $13.8 trillion in interest alone.

That's $4.3 trillion more than the entire projected defense budget over the same period.

Here's the trap:

Higher debt forces more borrowing. More borrowing raises rates. Higher rates increase interest payments. Which requires more borrowing.

The US is in a debt spiral in slow motion.

And almost nobody is talking about it.
