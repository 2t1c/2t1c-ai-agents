# Negative Examples — What This Writing Must Never Look Like

These are examples of what NOT to produce. Each one is labeled with the failure pattern and a diagnosis.

Read these before writing anything. If your output resembles any of these patterns, stop and rewrite.

---

## Failure Pattern 1 — Choppy Same-Length Rhythm

Every sentence is the same length. Nothing builds. Nothing lands harder than anything else. The reader's eye moves at the same pace the whole way through and feels nothing.

**Example of this failure:**

The French Academy rejected Marie Curie.

She had two Nobel Prizes.

This is a shocking fact.

The system failed her.

But her legacy survived.

Her ideas changed medicine forever.

She is celebrated today.

Her story shows us about perseverance.

**Why it fails:** Every sentence is the same length. The rhythm is flat. Nothing builds toward anything. "Shocking fact" tells the reader what to feel instead of triggering the feeling. "Shows us about perseverance" is a motivational closer — it explains the meaning instead of letting it land.

**The fix:** Vary the length. Let some paragraphs build across two or three sentences. Save the short lines for the moments that earn them.

---

## Failure Pattern 2 — Bullets as the Main Content Delivery

**Example of this failure:**

Rosalind Franklin's contributions to science:
- Took Photo 51, the key X-ray image of DNA
- Did 100 hours of exposure work
- Had her data shared without permission
- Never received credit
- Died before the Nobel was awarded

Her story teaches us that:
- Women face bias in science
- Credit matters
- We should acknowledge unrecognized contributions

**Why it fails:** Bullets flatten everything to the same level. The theft of her data, the specificity of Photo 51, the cruelty of dying before the recognition — all of it becomes equal in a bulleted list. The drama disappears. The voice disappears. It reads like a Wikipedia summary, not writing.

**The fix:** Write it as narrative. Let each fact arrive in sequence. The reader should feel the story building.

## Failure Pattern 3 — Motivational Closer

**Examples of this failure:**

"Her story shows us that with persistence, anything is possible."

"The lesson here is clear: never give up."

"This is why we should always strive to do our best."

"Be extraordinary."

"Take care!"

"Go be great today."

**Why it fails:** This is the old GeniusGTX voice. It tells the reader what to think and feel. It turns a story about something real into a generic life lesson. The reader has heard this a thousand times. It registers as filler.

**The fix:** End on a truth. Not an encouragement. Not a lesson. A statement that crystallizes what the piece was actually about. Leave it alone. The reader finishes the thought themselves.

Correct: "That's the pattern you find when you look at the record honestly. The institution rejects the person. The idea survives anyway."

## Failure Pattern 4 — Explained Metaphors

**Example of this failure:**

"Think of expertise like a lens. The more powerful the lens becomes, the narrower its field of view. In other words, as you get better at something, you start to miss things you used to be able to see — because your brain is now filtering everything through the patterns it knows. This is exactly what the Einstellung Effect describes."

**Why it fails:** The metaphor (the lens) is explained before it can do any work. "In other words" appears, which is the clearest possible signal that the writer doesn't trust the reader. The metaphor and the explanation cancel each other out. Neither lands.

**The fix:** Either use the metaphor and trust it to carry the idea, or drop the metaphor and state the idea directly. Never do both in the same paragraph.

## Failure Pattern 5 — Filler Openers

**Examples of this failure:**

"Throughout history, there have been many examples of..."

"In today's world, it's more important than ever to..."

"It's important to note that..."

"One could argue that..."

"As we all know..."

"It's no secret that..."

**Why it fails:** These phrases signal that the writer doesn't trust their opening line enough to let it stand alone. They pad the front of the sentence with noise. Every reader has learned to skip them.

**The fix:** Start with the thing. Not the setup for the thing. The thing.

Wrong: "It's no secret that Marie Curie faced enormous challenges as a woman in science."
Right: "In 1934, the French Academy of Sciences voted against admitting Marie Curie as a member."

## Failure Pattern 6 — Over-Segmentation (Every Sentence Its Own Line)

**Example of this failure:**

Rosalind Franklin discovered the structure of DNA.

But she never got credit.

Watson and Crick took her work.

They published it without her.

She died before the Nobel was given.

Her name was erased.

But now we know the truth.

Her work was essential.

She deserves to be remembered.

**Why it fails:** When every single sentence is its own paragraph, nothing is more important than anything else. The format is the same as a tweet thread from 2019 — it reads as content production, not writing. There's no rhythm because nothing ever builds. The reader processes a series of equal-weight facts rather than a developing piece of writing.

**The fix:** Let related sentences live together in the same paragraph. Some paragraphs can be one line. But some must be two or three sentences that build a thought together before moving to the next idea.

## Failure Pattern 7 — Formulaic Narrator Phrases

**Example of this failure:**

"This is where it gets interesting..."

"But wait — there's more..."

"Here's the part nobody tells you..."

"Stay with me on this one..."
[three paragraphs later]
"This is where it gets really interesting..."
[two paragraphs later]
"I promise this is going somewhere..."

**Why it fails:** Narrator phrases are connective tissue. They work when they arrive once or twice in a piece at the exact moment they're earned. When they appear repeatedly, or when they're inserted mechanically regardless of what surrounds them, the reader notices the formula and checks out. The phrase signals that something interesting is coming. If that signal appears too often, the reader stops believing it.

**The fix:** Use 2-3 narrator phrases per full piece maximum. Each one must be earned by what came before it. If the content doesn't earn the narrator phrase, the narrator phrase shouldn't be there.

---

## Failure Pattern 8 — The "It Isn't A, It's B" Structure Used Repeatedly

**Example of this failure:**

"The issue isn't about intelligence. It's about systems."

[two paragraphs later]

"The problem isn't the technology. It's the incentive structure."

[two paragraphs later]

"This isn't about failure. It's about how we define success."

**Why it fails:** The first instance works — it reframes the reader's assumption and creates a useful contrast. The second instance feels slightly weaker. The third instance signals that the writer has a habit, not a technique. The structure becomes a template the reader is completing before you finish the sentence.

**The fix:** Use it once per piece maximum. After the first instance, rewrite any subsequent contrast as a positive statement that leads with what something IS.

---

## The Two Universal Failure Patterns

Every piece of weak output traces back to one of two root causes:

**Root Cause 1 — Not trusting the reader.**
Explaining the metaphor. Summarizing what was just said. Telling the reader what to feel. Ending with a lesson rather than a truth. All of these are the writer doing work the reader should be allowed to do themselves.

**Root Cause 2 — Not varying the rhythm.**
When every sentence is the same length, or every paragraph is one line, or every section follows the same structural pattern — the writing feels produced rather than written. The fix is almost always: let some paragraphs build, and let the short lines land after them.
