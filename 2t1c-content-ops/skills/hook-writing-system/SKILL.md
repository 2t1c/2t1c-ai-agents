# 🪝 Hook Writing Guide

The complete reference for writing hooks on GeniusGTX content. This guide covers angle selection, hook anatomy, emotional triggers, language choices, and narrator voice.

Every post has two halves: the **hook** and the **body**. This guide governs the hook. The ✍️ Writing Style Guide governs the body. Agents must read both before producing any content.

---

## Part 1 — Angle Finding

The angle is the creative decision that determines whether the hook works or fails. Everything after the angle is assembly.

### The Angle vs The Topic

**Topic** = the subject matter. Marie Curie. The Butterfly Effect. The Cold War.

**Angle** = the specific entry point that makes the topic surprising. Not "Marie Curie the scientist" but "the institution she built her life around actively destroyed her."

One topic can have multiple angles. The angle selection is the creative decision.

### The Angle Formula

**Single Authority:** [Well-known figure or institution] + [unexpected visible action or association]. The unexpected action must be visualizable. Not a concept. A scene.

**Double Authority:** [Well-known figure/entity A] + [unexpected association with well-known figure/entity B]. Can be person + person, person + institution, person + country, person + ideology. Authority transfers both ways. Hard limit: two authorities maximum.

### The Angle Strength Spectrum

**Level 4 — Double Authority + Primal Trigger.** Two recognizable entities. Unexpected association. Hits at least one primal trigger. Examples: Turing + British Government. Tesla + Westinghouse.

**Level 3 — Single Authority + Implication.** One well-known figure. Unexpected visible action. Reader feels personal resonance. Examples: Marie Curie's radioactive notebooks. Dr. Seuss training soldiers.

**Level 2 — Single Authority, No Implication.** One well-known figure. Unexpected visible action. No direct reader resonance. Hook needs to work harder.

**Level 1 — No Authority Yet.** Requires the Expansion Move before any hook can be written.

### The Three Filters (All Must Pass)

**Filter 1 — The Unexpected Association.** Is there a connection between two recognizable things the reader wouldn't have put together?

**Filter 2 — The Visualization Test.** Can you describe the core moment in one sentence and picture it clearly? If yes, angle is ready. If no, run the Expansion Move.

**Filter 3 — The Primal Trigger Test.** Which trigger does it activate? Betrayal by someone trusted. Survival against impossible odds. Love or loyalty under extreme pressure. Vulnerability behind power. Sacrifice with massive consequence. Justice or injustice at extreme scale. Hit one = strong. Hit two = exceptional.

### The Expansion Move

When the angle feels weak or thin, run these moves in order:

**Move 1 — Find the authority.** Most recognizable name or institution connected to this topic.

**Move 2 — Find the unexpected action.** What did that entity do that contradicts what the reader would expect? Not the achievement. The contradiction.

**Move 3 — Find the secondary ring.** What unexpected facts exist one layer removed from the obvious story? The real angle often hides here.

**Move 4 — Look for the second authority.** Is there a second well-known figure or institution that connects to the first in an unexpected way?

**Move 5 — Visualize the scene.** Can you describe it in one sentence and someone can close their eyes and see it? If yes, proceed. If no, repeat from Move 3.

### The Rule

**Never start assembling a hook until the angle passes the Visualization Test.**

## Part 2 — Hook Anatomy

The hook is the first visible unit of any post. On X, the hook must land within the first 280 characters before truncation. Everything after 300 characters can run as long as the content needs.

### Opener Types

**Type A — Superlative Claim.** The highest possible declaration. Always absolute, never hedged. Write "the greatest" not "one of the greatest." Example: "No one made more money in trading than Jim Simons."

**Type B — Year Anchor.** Specific year + vivid low point + unexpected comparison. Write as a statement, not a setup. Example: "In 1978, China was poorer than Chad, Bangladesh, and Malawi."

**Type C — Named Person + Immediate Conflict.** Name + age or role + conflict verb against something larger. Example: "Markus Villig was 19 when he declared war on Uber and Lyft."

**Type D — Contrarian Challenge.** Named authority + withheld truth. Always name a specific trusted institution. Example: "Everyone talks about the same geniuses. But for me, there's one woman who stands head and shoulders above all."

**Type E — Quote Drop.** High-status name + transgressive quote + personal pivot. The quote must feel slightly dangerous to conventional wisdom.

**Type F — Recency Signal.** Time word + name + action. Creates urgency. Example: "80,000 secret JFK assassination files just dropped."

**Type G — Absurd Setup.** Situation + absurd twist delivered immediately. The absurdity IS the hook.

**Type H — Direct Address.** Opens by pointing at a subject cinematically. Example: "Look at this man."

**Type I — Personal Observation.** Writer leads with a pattern they've noticed. Specificity of the group matters.

**Type J — Direct Confrontation.** A direct punch with no setup. The accusation itself is the hook.

**Type K — Personal Obsession.** Writer leads with their own fixation as the credibility signal. Then immediately defines the subject in one loaded sentence.

**Type L — Personal Voice ("I" Statement).** The writer's own experience IS the credibility. Sub-types: L1 Belief Reversal, L2 Obsession Declaration, L3 Personal Discovery, L4 Identity Marker, L5 Pattern Observer, L6 Credential Pivot.

**Type M — Narrative Frame.** Applies a superlative to the narrative itself before a single fact is given. Example: "The greatest plot twist in America's AI war with China:"

### Escalation Types

**Type A — Negation Staircase.** Three names by ascending prestige, each on its own line. Last name lands hardest.

**Type B — Dismissal Before Reversal.** Named skeptic + dismissal verb. Always name WHO laughed.

**Type C — Stacked Micro-Facts.** Multiple devastating facts compressed into ultra-short lines. Each line gets shorter.

**Type D — Contrast Jump.** Specific number + comparison unit + contrast number. Both sides always named. The gap must feel offensive or absurd.

### Closer Types (Hook Closer, Not Post Closer)

The closer is the sentence that transitions the hook into the body. It never summarizes. It only opens the door.

**Type A — Plain Delivery Promise.** "Here's how [NAME] did it:"

**Type B — Numbered Delivery Promise.** "Here are [N] [adjective] insights:"

**Type C — Benefit-Framed Promise.** "Once you understand how X, you'll Y."

**Type D — Personal Reaction CTA.** Writer inserts genuine voice. "I swear this has to be the wildest rescue operation in espionage history."

**Type E — Personal Admission.** Writer admits uncertainty. "I genuinely don't know how this ends:"

**Type F — The Introduction.** Cinematic. Names the subject like you're being ushered into a room.

**Type G — Suspended Tension.** Freezes the reader inside collective anticipation.

### The Pivot Phrase

Every hook has exactly one pivot. The sentence that turns "here's the problem" into "here's why you should read on."

Approved pivot verbs: rewrote, exposed, transformed, revealed, collapsed, disrupted, bankrupt, seized, terrified, refused, buried, unleashed.

Never use as pivot verbs: decided to, started to, tried to, worked on, began.

### Stakes Injector

The line that introduces personal or moral risk. Appears just before or inside the pivot. Exaggerate the consequence, not the fact. Always use "nearly" to keep the exaggeration believable.

Formula: "But what [subject] did next nearly [catastrophic consequence] forever."

## Part 3 — Emotional Triggers & Language

### The 5 Emotional Triggers

**1. Fear of Being Left Behind.** Reader suspects the world is moving and they're not. Key words: quietly, nobody's talking about, before anyone else, most overlooked, silently, while everyone's focused on.

**2. Curiosity Gap.** Brain cannot tolerate an open loop. Key words: what he did next, the real reason, what really happened, you'll never see X the same.

**3. Institutional Betrayal.** Validates latent distrust toward authorities. Key words: won't tell you, fought to keep hidden, exposed, finally, secret, the truth behind.

**4. Awe at Scale.** Subject defied the laws of reality. Numbers are the weapon. Key words: greatest, insane, mind-blowing, impossible, [number] in [short timeframe].

**5. Identity Mirror.** The story is a reflection of the reader's own life or fear. Key words: your, every X must, what you need to know, once you understand.

### Strongest Pairings

Fear of Being Left Behind + Identity Mirror = most urgent. Curiosity Gap + Awe at Scale = most addictive. Institutional Betrayal + Identity Mirror = most anger-driven.

### Credibility Rules

Numbers must be exact and comparative. Not "billions" but "$7.4 BILLION." Not "quickly" but "in 28 days." Not "very poor" but "poorer than Chad, Bangladesh, and Malawi."

Speed reveals always use "just." "Collapsed in just 28 days" not "collapsed in 28 days." The word "just" makes speed feel violent, not merely factual.

Adversarial verbs always active and named. Never "Brazil's rubber dominance eventually ended." Always "The British sent a spy to destroy it."

### Loaded Verb Bank

Approved: declared war, pulled off, manipulated, exposed, vanished, collapsed, bankrupted, disrupted, rewrote, slashed, seized, terrified, revealed, refused, buried, unleashed, dismantled, transformed, destroyed, hijacked.

Never use as main action verbs: started, worked on, tried, decided, began, made progress, helped, contributed, played a role in.

## Part 4 — Narrator Voice Phrases

Use 2-3 per post maximum. Position determines the job, not the phrase itself. Any phrase can open a hook, appear mid-post, or close.

### Job 1 — Pacing Reset (after dense information sections)

Stay with me on this one... Bear with me. This gets wild. Read every line of this carefully... Give me 60 seconds and I'll change how you see this forever...

### Job 2 — Anticipation Builder (before a major reveal)

This is where the story completely changes... This is the detail that changes everything... This is the part nobody tells you... And then it gets even better...

### Job 3 — Credibility Through Vulnerability (admit uncertainty or surprise)

I genuinely don't know how this ends... This kept me up at night... I went down a rabbit hole on this and never came back... This completely changed how I think about...

### Job 4 — Permission to Feel (after a surprising fact lands)

This broke my brain when I first found it... This shouldn't be possible. But it happened. I've read this a dozen times and it still doesn't feel real...

### Job 5 — Stakes Elevation (before the close or final insight)

This matters more than most people realize... The world looks different after you understand this... File this one away. It's going to matter. The surface story is interesting. What's underneath it is everything...

## Part 5 — Formatting Rules (Non-Negotiable)

One sentence per line in the hook. No em dashes. Split into new sentences instead.

Short sentences hit harder when they stand alone.

Scroll-stop moment must land within first 200-300 characters. The 280-character context: first 280 must carry the pull before Twitter truncates.

The closer is not low-leverage. Use the best possible closer, not the shortest.

### The Contrast Construction Principle

[What everyone assumes] + [The reality is the opposite] + [One specific vivid detail that proves it]. The contrast creates the curiosity gap. The vivid detail makes it undeniable.

### The Prioritization Principle

Before writing: "What is the ONE thing readers would feel most cheated for not knowing?" Not the most impressive fact. Not the most dramatic. The one that creates the biggest gap between what the reader assumed was true and what actually happened. Lead with the gap, not the achievement.
