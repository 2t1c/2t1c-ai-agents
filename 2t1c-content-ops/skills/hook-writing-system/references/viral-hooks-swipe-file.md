## NEGATIVE EXAMPLES (Learn From — Do Not Replicate)

### Dancing Plague — Original (Underperformed)

In 1518, an entire city lost control of their bodies...
The cause? Something far more mysterious than any disease.
Welcome to the Dancing Plague and its 500-year-old mystery: 🧵

**Why it failed:** Abstract teases with no physical detail. "Lost control of their bodies" and "more mysterious than any disease" ask the reader to trust without showing them why. The concrete detail — people hired musicians to keep them dancing until they died — is completely absent. The closer names the subject instead of creating a gap.

**Pattern:** Prioritization failure. The most interesting detail was never surfaced.

### Flying Aircraft Carrier — Original (Underperformed)

In 1929, the U.S. Navy built a weapon even Stan Lee couldn't imagine.
This was America's secret answer to Japan's rising naval power.
Here's the story of a flying fortress the size of THREE cities and what happened next… 🧵

**Why it failed:** "The size of THREE cities" is not a visual measurement — cities have no fixed size. The caps on THREE add emphasis to a comparison that doesn't land. **Pattern:** Visual/Active failure. Abstract size comparison replaced a concrete physical detail.

---

## Two Patterns Across All Negative Examples

**Pattern 1:** The most interesting detail is almost never leading. It's buried in the middle or the closer. The fix is almost always the same — find the single most surprising specific fact and move it to line 1 or 2.

**Pattern 2:** Vague pivot lines ("changed science forever," "mysteriously disappeared," "what happened next") are doing work that specific details should be doing. Every time a hook relies on a vague pivot, it signals the angle hasn't been fully found yet.
