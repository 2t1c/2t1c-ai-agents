# Hook Anatomy Reference

## Opener Types

**Type A — Superlative Claim**
The highest possible declaration. Always absolute, never hedged.
Rule: Write "the greatest" not "one of the greatest."
Example: "No one made more money in trading than Jim Simons."
Example: "This man was the greatest spy in history."

**Type B — Year Anchor**
Specific year + vivid low point + unexpected comparison.
Rule: Write as a statement, not a setup. It carries its own weight.
Example: "In 1978, China was poorer than Chad, Bangladesh, and Malawi."
Example: "In 2008, Elon pulled off the greatest comeback in corporate history."

**Type C — Named Person + Immediate Conflict**
Name + age or role + conflict verb against something larger.
Example: "Markus Villig was 19 when he declared war on Uber and Lyft."

**Type D — Contrarian Challenge**
Named authority + withheld truth. Always name a specific trusted institution.
Example: "Your doctor won't tell you this about anxiety."
Example: "Everyone talks about the same geniuses. But for me, there's one woman who stands head and shoulders above all."

**Type E — Quote Drop**
High-status name + transgressive quote + personal pivot.
The quote must feel slightly dangerous to conventional wisdom.
Example: "Nassim Taleb once said: 'The three most harmful addictions are heroin, carbohydrates, and a monthly salary.'"

**Type F — Recency Signal**
Time word + name + action. Creates urgency.
Example: "80,000 secret JFK assassination files just dropped."
Example: "Yesterday, Sam Altman shared mind-blowing new insights on AI at Stanford."

**Type G — Absurd Setup**
Situation + absurd twist delivered immediately. The absurdity IS the hook.
Example: "In 1980, when 6 Americans were trapped in Iran, the CIA had a crazy idea: Send in an agent posing as a Hollywood producer to create a Star Wars ripoff called Argo."

**Type H — Direct Address**
Opens by pointing at a subject cinematically.
Example: "Look at this man."
Example: "This is Charles Ingram."

**Type I — Personal Observation**
Writer leads with a pattern they've noticed. Specificity of the group matters.
Example: "One pattern I've noticed in all miserable men:"

**Type J — Direct Confrontation**
A direct punch with no setup. The accusation itself is the hook.
Example: "You've been lied to."
Example: "Your 9-to-5 job is dying."

**Type K — Personal Obsession**
Writer leads with their own fixation as the credibility signal.
Then immediately defines the subject in one loaded sentence.
Example: "I'm obsessed with cognitive biases."

**Type L — Personal Voice ("I" Statement)**
The writer's own experience IS the credibility. Most vulnerable to voice mismatch.

Sub-types:
- L1 Belief Reversal: "I used to think X. Then Y changed everything."
- L2 Obsession Declaration: "I'm obsessed with X."
- L3 Personal Discovery: "I have spent [time] uncovering the truth about X."
- L4 Identity Marker: "I'm [identity]. [Specific observation only that identity would make]."
- L5 Pattern Observer: "One pattern I've noticed in [specific group]:"
- L6 Credential Pivot: "As a [credential], I hate to break it to you, but..."

**Type M — Narrative Frame**
Applies a superlative to the narrative itself before a single fact is given.
Example: "The greatest plot twist in America's AI war with China:"

---

## Note on Types A and B
Write as a statement, not a setup. It should carry its own weight before anything else arrives.
All other opener types can and should be setups that earn their payoff in the lines that follow.

## Escalation Types

**Type A — Negation Staircase**
Three names by ascending prestige, each on its own line. Last name lands hardest.
Example: "Not Buffett. Not Soros. Not Dalio."
Rule: Use 3 names minimum. Arrange from less to more prestigious.

**Type B — Dismissal Before Reversal**
Named skeptic + dismissal verb. Always name WHO laughed.
Example: "His competitors laughed. 6 months later, they lost $23 billion."

**Type C — Stacked Micro-Facts**
Multiple devastating facts compressed into ultra-short lines. Each line gets shorter.
Example: "Tesla was burning $4M per month on a car no one wanted. 0 sold. 3 weeks from bankruptcy."
Rule: The compression creates urgency. Each line must be shorter than the last.

**Type D — Contrast Jump**
Specific number + comparison unit + contrast number. Both sides always named.
Example: "A senior engineer in Paris makes $65K. The same role in Silicon Valley? $320K."
Rule: The gap must feel offensive or absurd.

## Closer Types

Rule: The closer never summarizes. It only opens the door.
Rule: Use the best possible closer — not the shortest.

**Type A — Plain Delivery Promise**
"Here's how [NAME] did it:"
"Here's the forgotten story:"
"Here's the full story:"

**Type B — Numbered Delivery Promise**
"Here are [N] [adjective] insights:"
"[N] key highlights you can't miss:"
"Let me save you 3 hours — here are the [N] most important things:"

**Type C — Benefit-Framed Promise**
"Once you understand how X, you'll Y."
"Here's what you need to know and how you should prepare:"
"Here's the story — and what it means for every [reader identity]:"

**Type D — Personal Reaction CTA**
Writer inserts genuine voice. Disarms skepticism.
"I swear this has to be the wildest rescue operation in espionage history."
"I genuinely can't believe this is real — here's the full story:"

**Type E — Personal Admission**
Writer admits uncertainty. Builds trust by refusing to wrap up neatly.
"I genuinely don't know how this ends:"
"I went down a rabbit hole on this and never came back:"

**Type F — The Introduction**
Cinematic. Names the subject like you're being ushered into a room.
Works when the subject is unfamiliar enough that naming it creates curiosity.
"Welcome to the Antikythera Mechanism: 🧵"

**Type G — Suspended Tension**
Freezes the reader inside collective anticipation. Named authority is currently paralyzed.
"But his first move has Silicon Valley holding its breath:"

## Stakes Injector

The line that introduces personal or moral risk. Appears just before or inside the pivot.

Proven constructions:
- "A move so risky it almost landed him in prison."
- "But what they created next nearly destroyed physics forever."
- "Congress saw the results and buried the program so completely it stayed hidden for 30 years."
- "The military classified everything about it the moment it went down."

Exaggeration Within Means rule: Exaggerate the consequence, not the fact.
Always use "nearly" to keep the exaggeration believable.
Formula: "But what [subject] did next nearly [catastrophic consequence] forever."

---

## The Pivot Phrase

The sentence that turns "here's the problem" into "here's why you should read on."
Every hook has exactly one pivot.

Approved pivot verbs: rewrote, exposed, transformed, revealed, collapsed, disrupted, bankrupt, seized, terrified, refused, buried, unleashed

Never use as pivot verbs: decided to, started to, tried to, worked on, began

Proven pivot constructions:
- "But what he did next rewrote the [domain] forever."
- "But then [NAME] made a move so risky..."
- "But instead of backing down..."
- "Once you understand how [NAME] [action], you'll [benefit]."

---

## The Middle Section Rule

The middle of the hook raises tension and anchors the scale of implication.
It does NOT explain the detail or justify why it's interesting.
It makes the reader feel the weight of what just happened before they know the full story.

Weak middle: "He discovered that radiation was affecting her health."
Strong middle: "She handled this material daily for 40 years — with bare hands."

The middle line should answer: "What is the scale of what happened here?"
Not: "What happened?"
