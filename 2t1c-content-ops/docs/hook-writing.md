## The Hook is Compositional

A hook is **not** a single type. It is a composition of multiple parts that stack inside one short unit:

```
SETUP → STAKES → PIVOT → PROMISE
```

This is the spine. ~95% of viral hooks follow it. Every hook GeniusGTX writes must pass this 4-beat test.

| Beat | Job | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **SETUP** (line 1) | Who/what/when. Under 20 words. Scroll-stop with specific number + primal trigger | *"In 1978, China was poorer than Chad, Bangladesh, and Malawi."* |
| **STAKES** (line 2-3) | Scale, cost, or odds — specific numbers | *"It had a GDP per capita of $155, a tiny fraction of the US's $10,000+."* |
| **PIVOT** (line 4) | "But", "Then", "Here's why" — the turn | *"But then Deng Xiaoping took power..."* |
| **PROMISE** (line 5) | Door into the body | *"45 years later, China is a $17 trillion superpower."* |

If a hook lacks one of these four beats, it feels off.

## Hook Length

| Format | Hook unit length | Line 1 ceiling |
|---|---|---|
| Tuki QRT / thread | Under 280 chars | Under 20 words |
| Long-form single post | 250–300 chars | Under 20 words |

**Line 1 hard ceiling: 20 words.** This is the scroll-stop. Lines 2-5 can be longer but must stay tight.

---

## Format-Adapted Closer (Promise beat)

This is the only thing that differs between formats:

- **All formats → explicit closer.** Tuki QRT, thread, AND long-form single post. Always use *"Here's how he did it:" / "Here's the full story:" / "Here's the part the [authority] has known since [year]:" / "A thread"*. Match the swipe file exactly. Never use an implicit closer.

**Example (explicit door — use this shape in every format):**

> "In 2008, Elon pulled off the greatest comeback in corporate history.
> Tesla was burning $4M per month on a car no one wanted. 0 sold. 3 weeks from bankruptcy.
> Then he made a move so risky it almost landed him in prison.
> **Here's the crazy story:**"

## Angle Filters (run before writing the hook)

The hook must pass at least **one** of these psychological filters:

1. **Identity Attack** — Does the hook threaten who the reader thinks they are? Does it challenge their self-image?
2. **Time Collapse** — Is there a same number or fact across two different eras? The gap between generations IS the hook.
3. **Direct Address** — Can you use "you" or "your" to make the reader the subject, not the observer?

And all three structural filters:

1. **Unexpected Association** — Connection between two recognizable things the reader wouldn't have put together.
2. **Visualization Test** — Can you describe the core moment in one sentence and picture it clearly? **REQUIRED.**
3. **Primal Trigger Test** — Does it activate one of: betrayal, survival, love under pressure, vulnerability behind power, sacrifice, injustice at scale? Hit one = strong. Hit two = exceptional.

If the angle fails Visualization, run the **Expansion Move**:
1. Find the authority — most recognizable name or institution connected to this topic
2. Find the unexpected action — what did that entity do that contradicts what the reader would expect?
3. Find the secondary ring — what unexpected facts exist one layer removed from the obvious story?
4. Look for a second authority — is there a second well-known figure that connects to the first in an unexpected way?
5. Visualize the scene — can someone close their eyes and see it?

**Never start writing the hook until the angle passes the Visualization Test.**

## Opener Types (the vocabulary)

These are **building blocks**, not exclusive categories. A real hook often combines 2-4 of these in one unit. Use the swipe file ([swipe-file.md](./swipe-file.md)) for proven combinations.

### Type A — Superlative Claim
Absolute declaration, never hedged. Write "the greatest" not "one of the greatest."
- *"No one made more money in trading than Jim Simons."*
- *"This man was the greatest spy in history."*

### Type B — Year Anchor
Specific year + vivid low point + unexpected comparison. Write as a statement, not a setup.
- *"In 1978, China was poorer than Chad, Bangladesh, and Malawi."*
- *"In 2008, Elon pulled off the greatest comeback in corporate history."*

### Type C — Named Person + Immediate Conflict
Name + age or role + conflict verb against something larger.
- *"Markus Villig was 19 when he declared war on Uber and Lyft."*

### Type D — Contrarian Challenge
Named authority + withheld truth. Always name a specific trusted institution.
- *"Your doctor won't tell you this about anxiety."*
- *"Everyone talks about the same geniuses. But for me, there's one woman who stands head and shoulders above all."*

### Type E — Quote Drop
High-status name + transgressive quote + personal pivot. The quote must feel slightly dangerous to conventional wisdom.
- *Nassim Taleb once said: "The three most harmful addictions are heroin, carbohydrates, and a monthly salary."*

**Use only when the quote is genuinely scroll-stopping on its own.** If the quote is merely descriptive, use Type F instead.

### Type F — Recency Signal
Time word + name + action. Creates urgency.
- *"80,000 secret JFK assassination files just dropped."*
- *"Yesterday, Sam Altman shared mind-blowing new insights on AI at Stanford."*

### Type G — Absurd Setup
Situation + absurd twist delivered immediately. The absurdity IS the hook.
- *"In 1980, when 6 Americans were trapped in Iran, the CIA had a crazy idea: Send in an agent posing as a Hollywood producer to create a Star Wars ripoff called Argo."*

### Type H — Direct Address
Opens by pointing at a subject cinematically.
- *"Look at this man."*
- *"This is Charles Ingram."*

### Type I — Personal Observation
Writer leads with a pattern they've noticed. Specificity of the group matters.
- *"One pattern I've noticed in all miserable men:"*

### Type J — Direct Confrontation
A direct punch with no setup. The accusation IS the hook.
- *"You were lied to about how the middle class was destroyed."*

### Type K — Personal Obsession
Writer leads with their own fixation as the credibility signal.
- *"I'm obsessed with cognitive biases."*
- *"I've spent 6 months reading Marcus Aurelius every morning."*

### Type L — Personal Voice ("I" Statement)
Writer's own experience IS the credibility.
- *"I was wrong about everything I believed about AI until last week."*
- *"I'm German. 16 years ago, the EU and US economies were neck and neck."*

### Type M — Narrative Frame
Applies a superlative to the narrative itself before a single fact is given.
- *"The greatest plot twist in America's AI war with China:"*

### Type N — Status Transfer
Underestimated person/group + elite concept/outcome + before elite authority. Activates identity mirror + institutional betrayal + awe at scale simultaneously.
- *"A Vietnamese textile merchant priced options correctly 16 years before Black-Scholes."*
- *"Three guys at a Canadian university beat every elite AI lab using two gaming graphics cards."*

### Compositional examples

A real hook stacks 2-4 types. Tag what's firing:

> "In 2012, three guys at a Canadian university beat every elite AI lab in the world using two gaming graphics cards."

| Part | Type | Job |
|---|---|---|
| "In 2012" | **Type B** | Year anchor |
| "three guys at a Canadian university" | **Type N** | Status transfer (underdogs) |
| "beat every elite AI lab in the world" | Middle (raises scale) | |
| "using two gaming graphics cards" | **Type G** | Absurd detail |

Four moves in 22 words. That's the machine.

## Escalation Types (how the hook builds)

After the opener, choose ONE escalation move:

### Type A — Negation Staircase
Three names by ascending prestige, each on its own line. Last name lands hardest.
- *"Not Buffett. Not Soros. Not Dalio."*

### Type B — Dismissal Before Reversal
Named skeptic + dismissal verb. Always name WHO laughed.
- *"His competitors laughed. 6 months later, they lost $23 billion."*

### Type C — Stacked Micro-Facts
Multiple devastating facts compressed into ultra-short lines. Each line shorter than the last.
- *"Tesla was burning $4M per month on a car no one wanted. 0 sold. 3 weeks from bankruptcy."*

### Type D — Contrast Jump
Specific number + comparison unit + contrast number. Both sides always named.
- *"A senior engineer in Paris makes $65K. The same role in Silicon Valley? $320K."*

The gap must feel offensive or absurd.

## The Pivot

Every hook has **exactly one pivot** — the sentence that turns "here's the problem" into "here's why you should read on."

### Approved pivot verbs

> rewrote, exposed, transformed, revealed, collapsed, disrupted, bankrupt, seized, terrified, refused, buried, unleashed

### Banned pivot verbs

Never use as pivot verbs:
> decided to, started to, tried to, worked on, began

### Proven pivot constructions

- *"But what he did next rewrote the [domain] forever."*
- *"But then [NAME] made a move so risky..."*
- *"But instead of backing down..."*
- *"Once you understand how [NAME] [action], you'll [benefit]."*

### Pivot verb guidance

Use the simplest accurate verb when the claim is already dramatic. Use a strong pivot verb when the claim needs the verb to carry the tension.

---

## Stakes Injector

The line that introduces personal or moral risk. Appears just before or inside the pivot.

### Proven constructions

- *"A move so risky it almost landed him in prison."*
- *"But what they created next nearly destroyed physics forever."*
- *"Congress saw the results and buried the program so completely it stayed hidden for 30 years."*
- *"The military classified everything about it the moment it went down."*

### Exaggeration Within Means rule

Exaggerate the consequence, not the fact. Always use **"nearly"** to keep the exaggeration believable.

Formula: *"But what [subject] did next nearly [catastrophic consequence] forever."*

---

## The Middle Section Rule

The middle of the hook **raises tension and anchors scale**. It does NOT explain the detail.

The middle line answers: *"What is the scale of what happened here?"*
NOT: *"What happened?"*

- Weak middle: "He discovered that radiation was affecting her health."
- Strong middle: "She handled this material daily for 40 years — with bare hands."

## Closer Types (the Promise beat)

The closer never summarizes. It only opens the door.
Use the **best** possible closer — not the shortest.

### Type A — Plain Delivery Promise
- *"Here's how [NAME] did it:"*
- *"Here's the forgotten story:"*

### Type B — Numbered Delivery Promise
- *"Here are [N] [adjective] insights:"*
- *"Let me save you 3 hours — here are the [N] most important things:"*

### Type C — Benefit-Framed Promise
- *"Once you understand how X, you'll Y."*
- *"Here's what you need to know and how you should prepare:"*

### Type D — Personal Reaction CTA
Writer inserts genuine voice. Disarms skepticism.
- *"I swear this has to be the wildest rescue operation in espionage history."*

### Type E — Personal Admission
Writer admits uncertainty. Builds trust.
- *"I genuinely don't know how this ends:"*
- *"I went down a rabbit hole on this and never came back:"*

### Type F — The Introduction
Cinematic. Names the subject like you're being ushered into a room.
- *"Welcome to the Antikythera Mechanism: 🧵"*

### Type G — Suspended Tension
Freezes the reader inside collective anticipation.
- *"But his first move has Silicon Valley holding its breath:"*

**Format note:** Types A-G are all **explicit** closers. Use them in every format — Tuki QRT, thread, and long-form single post alike. Never strip them.

## 5 Emotional Triggers

Every hook should hit at least one (ideally two):

### 1. Fear of Being Left Behind
Reader suspects the world is moving and they're not.
**Key words:** quietly, nobody's talking about, before anyone else, most overlooked, silently, while everyone's focused on

### 2. Curiosity Gap
Brain cannot tolerate an open loop.
**Key words:** what he did next, the real reason, what really happened, you'll never see X the same, the part nobody tells you

### 3. Institutional Betrayal
Validates latent distrust toward authorities.
**Key words:** won't tell you, fought to keep hidden, exposed, finally, secret, the truth behind, kept it hidden for X years

### 4. Awe at Scale
Subject defied the laws of reality. Numbers are the weapon.
**Key words:** greatest, insane, mind-blowing, impossible, [number] in [short timeframe], the largest in history

### 5. Identity Mirror
The story is a reflection of the reader's own life or fear.
**Key words:** your, every X must, what you need to know, once you understand, how you should prepare

### Strongest pairings

- **Fear of Being Left Behind + Identity Mirror** → most urgent
- **Curiosity Gap + Awe at Scale** → most addictive
- **Institutional Betrayal + Identity Mirror** → most anger-driven

## Credibility Rules

- **Numbers exact and comparative.** Not "billions" but "$7.4 BILLION." Not "quickly" but "in 28 days."
- **Speed reveals always use "just".** "Collapsed in just 28 days" — "just" makes speed feel violent, not merely factual.
- **Adversarial verbs always active and named.** Not "Brazil's rubber dominance eventually ended." Always "The British sent a spy to destroy it."
- **Superlatives absolute.** "The greatest" not "one of the greatest."

## 6 Content Categories (parallel to opener types)

Every hook sits in one topic-level mode. Pick the category as you write:

1. **Biographical Story** — individual arc, rise, fall, revelation (Jim Simons, Steve Jobs, Markus Villig)
2. **Historical / Geopolitical** — events, civilizations, systemic shifts (Göbekli Tepe, Singapore, Deng Xiaoping)
3. **Business / Strategy** — company moves, market disruptions (Reliance Jio, Apple's revival, Costco's psychology)
4. **Conceptual** — ideas, frameworks, mental models (Butterfly Effect, cognitive biases, nervous system regulation)
5. **Podcast Recap** — interview highlights (Sam Altman Stanford, Elon on Rogan, Milei with Lex)
6. **Personal Authority** — writer's credentials or contrarian stance ("I'm German", "I'm obsessed with...", therapist hooks)

See [swipe-file.md](./swipe-file.md) for proven hooks in each category.

## Format-Specific Defaults

### For Tuki QRT
- Default opener: **Type F (Recency Signal)** — time word + name + action
- Type E (Quote Drop) only when the quote stops the scroll on its own
- No siren emoji 🚨
- Use "→" facts in the body
- Explicit closer ("Here's how:", "A thread")

### For Long-Form Post
- Default opener: **Type B (Year Anchor)** when historical, **Type A (Superlative)** when biographical, **Type N (Status Transfer)** when underdog narrative
- Sourced from transcripts/podcasts → lean Type E or F
- Implicit closer (last line of hook unit opens body's first paragraph)
