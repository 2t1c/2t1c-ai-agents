## The Cornerstone — Universal Familiarity, Specialist Reveal

**Every post must pass this filter first.** All other idea-quality checks are secondary.

A strong GeniusGTX post is built on a subject the reader *already holds a mental model of* — a pyramid, a credit card, a hospital bed, a dinner table, an iPhone, a vaccine, a factory wage — and then reveals something about it that the specialist world knew but the reader never did.

### The test

> Can a 10-year-old picture the thing in the hook, with no context?
>
> AND: would a PhD in the field recognize the reveal as real and non-obvious?

Both yes → strong idea. Either no → skip.

### Why this is the cornerstone

- **Zero prerequisite knowledge.** Reader doesn't need to know what RFID, mRNA, or quasicrystals are to be gripped. They just need to care about the familiar surface.
- **Maximum "I had no idea"** The dopamine of the post comes from the gap between what the reader thought they knew and the specialist truth. The bigger the gap, the bigger the share.
- **Competition-free on the timeline.** News-pegged posts fight for the same eyeballs as 50 other takes. Universal-familiar posts compete against nothing.

### Canonical examples

| Post | Universal-familiar surface | Specialist reveal |
|---|---|---|
| Pyramid | Everyone's seen a pyramid | The ramp math doesn't work; builders used the pyramid as its own scaffold |
| Tap-to-pay | Every wallet has one | The tech descends from a 1945 Soviet spy bug |
| Chi-Chi's / Hep A | Restaurant chain everyone remembers | One $50 vaccine, one cook, 650 infected, chain dead in 30 days |
| Steve Jobs dinner table | Everyone owns an iPhone | Its inventor banned it from his own family |
| Duke silence study | Everyone sleeps, everyone hears | Silence grows new neurons; noise doesn't |
| Ford 1914 wages | Everyone has a job | The man who invented mass employment understood something his heirs forgot |
| Shechtman quasicrystals | Everyone's held a crystal (salt, diamond) | The rules of matter were wrong for a century |

### Anti-examples (posts that fail the cornerstone)

- *"The AI safety debate inside DeepMind's alignment team"* — insider-only surface
- *"Why the Treasury market's basis trade is unwinding"* — specialist-only surface
- *"A new CRISPR delivery method using lipid nanoparticles"* — specialist-only surface AND specialist-only reveal

A story can be true, important, and well-sourced and still fail this filter. That's fine — those belong on other accounts. GeniusGTX is a gallery for the greatest minds explained to the widest audience.

## The Four Supporting Traits

Once Universal Familiarity is met, favor ideas that hit MORE of these. 4/4 is ideal; 2/4 is the floor.

### 1. Lone named protagonist vs. established consensus

Not "scientists" — *Dan Shechtman*. Not "architects" — *Jean-Pierre Houdin, a French architect*. Named, with credentials, standing against a named opposing force.

**Why it works:** reader roots for the underdog. A faceless discovery doesn't generate loyalty. A named human being told they're wrong, then proven right, does.

### 2. Physical / evidentiary resolution

The story must end with *evidence that settled it*, not just argument. Muon radiography found the void. Electron diffraction showed the pattern. The outbreak killed the chain. Semmelweis's ratio of dead mothers dropped.

**Why it works:** the resolution feels earned, not asserted. No "maybe, we'll see." The territory won.

### 3. Teachable framework as the payoff

A portable mental model the reader leaves carrying. "Inversion." "Map vs. territory." "First-order vs. second-order." "Revealed preference." "You cannot automate the customer." The framework gets **named** at the close — not just implied.

**Why it works:** the post is not a fun fact. It's a tool. The reader uses it next Tuesday on an unrelated decision. That's why they come back, subscribe, buy the toolkit.

### 4. No expiration

The story could be posted any day of any year. Not news-pegged, not tied to a trending topic. Evergreen.

**Why it works:** it can be drafted in batches, scheduled in gaps, and revived months later. It doesn't go stale.

## How to apply this in content-generator Phase 1

When picking from the New pool:

1. **Cornerstone filter first.** For each candidate, answer the 10-year-old/PhD test out loud (in the report). If either side fails, skip — even if the card is tagged 🔴 Breaking.
2. **Score the four supporting traits** (0–4). Pick the highest scorers.
3. **Urgency is a tiebreaker, not the primary sort.** A 🟢 Evergreen card that hits 5/5 archetype beats a 🔴 Breaking card that hits 1/5. The pipeline is supposed to reward quality, not recency.
4. **If zero cards pass the cornerstone, self-source.** Don't draft a weak post just to fill the run. Generate a Pyramid-archetype idea from training + WebSearch and flag the refill-needed warning.

## Hunting grounds (for self-sourcing when the pipeline is empty)

Stories that reliably produce Universal-Familiar + Specialist-Reveal:

- **Famous objects with hidden histories** (tap-to-pay, GPS, Teflon, microwaves, Post-it notes, duct tape, Velcro, the zipper, the safety pin)
- **Famous companies with forgotten pivots** (Nintendo made playing cards, Shell was an actual seashell trader, Nokia made rubber boots, Samsung sold dried fish)
- **Famous inventions rejected at birth** (Semmelweis hand-washing, Wegener's continental drift, Barbara McClintock's jumping genes, Katalin Karikó's mRNA, Shechtman's quasicrystals, Dan Shechtman, Lynn Margulis's endosymbiosis)
- **Famous people who did something almost nobody knows** (Hedy Lamarr invented frequency-hopping; Isaac Newton ran the Royal Mint; Nikola Tesla wanted wireless power; Einstein refused to wear socks; Dr. Seuss made WWII propaganda)
- **Everyday phrases / objects with wild origins** (why wedding rings go on the 4th finger, why we say "steal one's thunder", why keyboards are QWERTY, why ties exist)
- **Famous events where the real cause is non-obvious** (the Tulip Mania wasn't what you think, the South Sea Bubble, the Great Emu War, the 1904 Olympic marathon)

When using these hunting grounds, verify every fact via WebSearch before writing. The archetype demands rigor; the reward depends on the reveal being true.
